Philosophy of Education

My educational vision and approach to transformative learning

Core Educational Beliefs

My philosophy of education is based on the belief that learning is a continuous process beginning at birth and extending beyond the classroom. Being from Chitral and having experienced education in Bangladesh, I understand that learning is shaped by family, society, and schools together. Education is not confined to institutions; it grows through lived experiences, relationships, and environment.

I believe education should focus on holistic development rather than only academic achievement. True education develops communication, problem-solving, critical thinking, and creativity. Learning should be meaningful and active—aligned with John Dewey's concept of learning through experience and Paulo Freire's problem-posing approach where students actively question and engage with knowledge.

The Living Tree of Learning

I imagine education as a living tree of learning. The child is at the center, like a growing seed. The roots represent the family, where values, language, and early habits are formed. The trunk represents the school, where structured learning and guidance take place. The branches represent society, where children interact with the wider world, develop identity, and gain exposure. Together, these parts nourish the child's growth, just as sunlight and water nourish a tree.

Learning is not linear but interconnected. If any part is weak, the growth of the child is affected. Therefore, education must be a shared responsibility among parents, schools, and society—a collaborative process that nurtures curiosity and helps children grow into confident, thoughtful, and capable individuals.

The Socratic Method in Modern Education

The Socratic method, developed by Socrates, uses questioning and dialogue rather than direct instruction. Students are guided to think deeply about their ideas, identify gaps in understanding, and achieve clearer thinking through reflection. This method promotes critical thinking, reflection, and self-awareness.

I believe the Socratic method should be used in Pakistani schools, especially at secondary and higher levels. Pakistan's education system heavily emphasizes memorization and exam-based learning, limiting creativity and analytical thinking. Research shows inquiry-based and questioning strategies significantly improve critical thinking and reasoning skills.

However, the method must align with developmental stages. Young children need structured instruction and guided learning. At secondary and higher levels, students are more capable of reasoning and discussion, making the Socratic method more effective. Importantly, this method fosters intellectual humility—helping learners recognize the limits of their knowledge and remain open to continuous learning.

Sociological Foundation: Identity & School

Schools are more than academic spaces; they are critical social environments where students develop identity, values, and behavior. From an interactionist perspective, identity is continuously shaped through everyday interactions. Students learn how to see themselves and behave through communication with teachers, peers, and institutional practices.

In Pakistan, schools shape identity through discipline systems, classroom structures, and examination practices. Students internalize values such as obedience, competition, and conformity. However, many experience "learning poverty"—unable to read and understand basic texts at expected levels—suggesting schooling focuses on memorization rather than meaningful understanding.

Everyday classroom practices—how feedback is given, how participation is encouraged, the labels students receive—significantly influence how students see themselves. This requires schools to become aware of their role in shaping identity and behavior, ensuring all students are supported in reaching their potential. Teachers' expectations and labeling carry lasting impacts on student self-concept and future behavior.

Portfolio Development Reflection

Insights from my learning journey

The development of this portfolio has been a gradual and reflective process. At the beginning, I found it challenging to connect abstract philosophical and sociological ideas with my personal understanding. However, as I progressed, I realized these theories are not only relevant but closely connected to real educational practices, especially in Pakistan's context.

Learning Through AI Assistance

A significant part of this process involved the thoughtful use of AI tools like ChatGPT. Rather than relying on AI for complete answers, I used it as a support system to organize my thoughts, refine language, and ensure alignment with instructions. This approach allowed me to maintain my voice while benefiting from academic guidance. My process involved understanding the question, drafting ideas in my own words, then refining with AI assistance—keeping me actively engaged in learning rather than dependent on technology.

Key Challenges & Growth

The main challenge was maintaining clarity while meeting academic requirements—word limits, citations, and alignment with specific perspectives. Through revision and reflection, I improved the quality of my work and learned to:

  • Critically engage with educational theories
  • Connect abstract concepts with real-life Pakistani contexts
  • Express ideas more clearly and academically
  • Use AI responsibly as a thinking tool, not a replacement
  • Develop stronger academic writing skills

Conclusion

This portfolio has taught me that learning is a continuous and reflective process. I have developed a deeper understanding of how to critically engage with theories, connect them with real-life contexts, and express ideas clearly. More importantly, I have strengthened my confidence as a learner and future educator committed to transformative educational practices.
